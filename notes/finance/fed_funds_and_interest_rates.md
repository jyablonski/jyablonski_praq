# Fed Funds Rate, Bank Rates, and the Economy

## The basic idea

The federal funds rate is the overnight interest rate that banks charge one another for lending balances held at the Federal Reserve.

The Federal Reserve (the Fed) does not usually set one exact market rate for every transaction. Its Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) announces a target range, and the Fed uses its tools to keep overnight rates near that range.

The fed funds rate is a policy rate: it is the starting point for many other short-term rates, but it is not the rate every household or business receives.

## How rates connect

| Rate or term | What it means | Usually affected by the fed funds rate? |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Federal funds rate | Overnight bank-to-bank lending rate and the Fed's main policy signal | Directly |
| Prime rate | Reference rate banks use for many business loans, HELOCs, and variable-rate credit | Closely; often moves with the fed funds rate |
| Savings account rate | Interest a bank pays on customer deposits | Usually, but also depends on bank competition and funding needs |
| CD rate | Interest paid on a deposit locked for a stated period | Yes, plus expectations for future rates and the CD's term |
| Credit card rate | Interest charged on revolving balances | Often; many cards use a variable rate tied to prime |
| Mortgage rate | Interest charged on a home loan | Indirectly; fixed mortgages are driven more by longer-term bond yields |
| Treasury and bond yields | Market-required return for lending to governments or companies | Indirectly; expectations for inflation, growth, and future Fed policy matter |

The transmission is not mechanical: a bank can lower or raise its deposit rate by less than the Fed's move, or not move it at all, depending on how badly it needs deposits and how much competition it faces.

## APR, APY, and compound interest

| Term | Applies primarily to | Meaning | Main comparison question |
| ----------------- | -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Interest rate | Borrowing or saving | The stated periodic or annual rate before considering compounding | What is the quoted rate? |
| APR | Borrowing | Annualized cost of credit; consumer-loan APR disclosures may include certain fees | What will this loan cost me per year? |
| APY | Saving or investing | Annualized yield including the effect of compounding | What will this deposit earn in one year if the rate stays constant? |
| Compound interest | Either | Interest earned on prior interest as well as on the original principal | How often is interest credited, and is it reinvested? |

For a principal `P`, nominal annual rate `r`, compounding `n` times per year, and `t` years: `A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)`.

For a nominal rate compounded `n` times per year: `APY = (1 + r/n)^n - 1`.

Example: $10,000 at a constant 4% APY earns about $400 in one year before taxes. A 4% nominal rate compounded monthly has an APY of about 4.07%, so the quoted label matters.

APR and APY are not interchangeable: APR is generally used to compare borrowing costs, while APY is generally used to compare deposit yields.

## What the Fed is trying to do

| Fed decision | Policy stance | Intended effect | Typical household and business response |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Raise the target range | Restrictive, or tighter | Reduce borrowing and demand to bring inflation down | Loans become more expensive; saving becomes more attractive; spending and investment may slow |
| Lower the target range | Accommodative, or looser | Encourage borrowing, demand, hiring, and investment | Loans become cheaper; deposit yields often fall; spending and investment may increase |
| Hold the target range | Steady | Wait for earlier policy to work or keep conditions balanced | Decisions depend more on expectations and other economic information |

The Fed is generally balancing its dual mandate of maximum employment and stable prices. It cannot directly control inflation or unemployment, and policy works with uncertain and often long lags.

## Inflation and unemployment

| Economic condition | Common policy pressure | Why |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Inflation is persistently too high | Raise rates or keep them high | Weaker demand can reduce pressure on prices and wages, while higher real returns encourage saving |
| Unemployment is rising and inflation is subdued | Lower rates or keep them lower | Cheaper credit can support consumption, business investment, production, and hiring |
| Inflation is high while unemployment is also rising | Difficult tradeoff | Tightening may help inflation but worsen employment; easing may help jobs but prolong inflation |
| Inflation and employment are near desired levels | Hold or adjust cautiously | Large moves could create unnecessary volatility |

The simple relationship is not one-to-one: higher rates can reduce demand and hiring, but unemployment also depends on technology, trade, fiscal policy, labor supply, and industry-specific shocks. Inflation can be caused by supply problems that interest rates cannot quickly fix.

## Does it affect the stock market?

Yes, but the direction is not guaranteed and the market usually reacts to expected future policy rather than only to the announcement.

| Channel | Higher fed funds rates | Lower fed funds rates |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Valuation | Higher discount rates usually reduce the present value investors assign to future profits | Lower discount rates usually support the value of future profits |
| Company financing | Debt costs rise; refinancing and new projects become harder | Debt costs fall; refinancing and new projects become easier |
| Consumer demand | Spending may weaken, reducing revenue for some companies | Spending may strengthen, supporting revenue for some companies |
| Alternative returns | Cash, CDs, and bonds become more competitive with stocks | Cash and bonds may look less attractive relative to stocks |
| Market signal | May indicate the Fed sees overheating or inflation risk | May indicate support for growth, or concern about economic weakness |

Stocks can fall after a rate cut if investors interpret it as evidence of a recession, and stocks can rise after a rate hike if economic growth and earnings are stronger than expected. Sector effects also differ: growth stocks are often more sensitive to discount rates, while banks may benefit from some higher-rate environments but can be hurt by weak loan demand or credit losses.

## Takeaway

The Fed controls a short-term policy lever, not every interest rate. Its rate changes flow through banks, credit markets, expectations, and financial conditions, influencing saving, borrowing, spending, employment, inflation, and asset prices. The intended direction is usually stimulus when rates fall and restraint when rates rise, but the actual outcome depends on the reason for the decision and how households, businesses, and markets respond.
