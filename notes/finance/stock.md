# Stock

## What It Is

Stocks represent ownership shares in companies. When you buy stock, you're purchasing a small piece of the business and become a shareholder.

Companies sell stock to raise money for growth, expansion, and operations. Instead of taking out loans from banks, they offer pieces of ownership to investors in exchange for capital.

The price of stock fluctuates based on supply and demand. If more people want to buy than sell, then the price goes up, and vice versa. This is also influenced by other factors like company perfromance, economic conditions, market news etc.

You can make money through stock by:

- Selling stock for more than what you paid for it
- Dividends, which are regular payments some companies make to shareholders.
- You can also lose money if stock prices fall below what you paid

Stocks are traded on exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ. These are marketplaces where buyers and sellers meet. But consumers typically buy and sell through a brokerage account rather than directly on the exchange.

### Why It Exists

The stock market exists to solve two fundamental problems. Companies need money to grow, and people with money want to put it to work. The stock market connects these two sides.

When a company wants to raise capital, it can sell ownership stakes (shares) to the public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO). Instead of taking on debt, the company gets cash upfront, and investors get a piece of the company's future earnings and growth. After the IPO, those shares trade on an exchange (like the NYSE or NASDAQ), which gives investors *liquidity* — the ability to buy or sell their stake whenever they want, rather than having their money locked up.

This liquidity is crucial. People would be far less willing to invest in companies if they couldn't exit their position. The secondary market (day-to-day trading) is what makes the primary market (companies raising capital) viable.

### How Stocks Are Priced

Stock pricing happens on two levels:

The mechanical level is pure supply and demand. At any moment, buyers place bids (what they'll pay) and sellers place asks (what they'll accept). When a bid and ask match, a trade executes and that becomes the current price. If more people want to buy than sell, the price gets pushed up, and vice versa.

The fundamental level is about what drives that supply and demand. In theory, a stock's "true" value is the present value of all future cash flows the company will generate for shareholders. This is called a discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation. The idea is simple: a dollar earned ten years from now is worth less than a dollar today (because of inflation, opportunity cost, and risk), so you discount future earnings back to present value.

In practice, investors use a mix of approaches to decide what a stock is worth:

Earnings-based metrics like the P/E ratio (price divided by earnings per share) let you compare how much you're paying per dollar of profit across companies. A stock trading at 15x earnings is "cheaper" than one at 40x, all else equal — but all else is rarely equal.

Growth expectations are often the biggest price driver. A company growing revenue 30% per year commands a higher multiple than one growing 5%, because investors are pricing in larger future cash flows. This is why tech stocks can trade at seemingly absurd valuations — the market is betting on future dominance.

Interest rates matter enormously. When rates are low, future earnings are worth more in today's dollars (lower discount rate), so stock prices rise. When the Fed raises rates, stocks tend to fall because those future cash flows get discounted more heavily, and safer investments like bonds become more attractive alternatives.

Sentiment and narrative also play a real role. Markets aren't perfectly rational. Fear, greed, momentum, and herd behavior can push prices well above or below any reasonable fundamental estimate — sometimes for extended periods.

### The Key Tension

There's an ongoing debate between two schools of thought. The efficient market hypothesis argues that stock prices already reflect all available information, so you can't consistently beat the market. The behavioral finance camp points out that markets are made up of humans (and algorithms built by humans) who are subject to biases, panic, and euphoria, creating mispricings that skilled investors can exploit.

The reality is probably somewhere in between. Markets are *mostly* efficient *most* of the time, but not perfectly so — which is what keeps active investors in business.

## Terms

1. Market Cap - Total value of all company shares (share price * number of outstanding shares). Determines if a company is small, mid, or large cap
1. Float - How many shares are available for public trading
1. Volume - How many shares trade daily, higher volume usually means easier to buy & sell
1. Bid / Ask Spread - The gap between what buyers offer and what sellers want, wider spread usually means higher trading costs
1. Dividend Yield - Annual dividend divided by stock price
1. Stock Splits - Company divides existing shares (2-for-1 split means you get 2 shares for every 1, but each worth half as much)
   - So if you owned 100 shares at $200 each ($20,000 total), after the split you'd have 200 shares at $100 each (still $20,000 total).
   - This makes sense for companies to do if their stock is like $100 per share, psychologically it makes a lot more sense to buy if a stock is much cheaper like $50 per share rather than $100, even though the total value of stock isnt changing whatsoever.
   - More outstanding shares means more trading activity, which companies usually prefer
   - Stock splits don't create any real value - the total value of your holdings stays exactly the same. But they can affect psychology and trading patterns.
1. Short Selling - Betting a stock will go down by borrowing and selling shares from a broker, and then buying them back later (hopefully when they're much cheaper) to return them to the broker
1. Margin trading - borrowing money to buy more stocks than you could with cash alone

## Private Stock

Private stock works on the same basic principle — ownership stakes in a company — but the mechanics are very different because there's no public exchange facilitating trades.

Private shares are typically held by founders, early employees (usually through stock options or RSUs), angel investors, and venture capital or private equity firms. The ownership structure is laid out in a cap table that tracks who owns what percentage.

### How Private Stock Gets Priced

Without a public market generating a price every second, private stock valuation is trickier and less transparent. It usually gets priced in a few ways:

Funding rounds are the most visible pricing events. When a VC firm invests $10M for 20% of a company, they've implicitly valued the whole company at $50M (the "post-money valuation"). But this is somewhat artificial — it reflects what *one* investor was willing to pay under specific terms, not a broad market consensus. These rounds also typically come with preferences (like liquidation preferences) that make VC shares worth more than common shares, so the headline valuation can be misleading.

409A valuations are independent appraisals that companies are required to get (usually annually or after major events) to set the fair market value of common stock. This is what determines the strike price for employee stock options. These valuations are almost always significantly lower than the latest funding round price, because common stock lacks the protections preferred shares have.

Secondary markets have emerged to add some liquidity. Platforms like Forge and EquityZen let employees and early investors sell private shares to accredited investors before an IPO. Prices here give a market-driven signal, but it's a thin, illiquid market, so discounts of 20-40% from the last funding round valuation aren't unusual.

### The Liquidity Problem

This is the biggest difference from public stocks. If you hold private shares, you often *can't* sell them even if you want to. Most companies have right of first refusal clauses and board approval requirements for any transfer. Employees can sit on paper wealth for years — sometimes a decade or more — with no way to realize it.

Your options for an eventual payout are generally limited to an IPO (company goes public), an acquisition (another company buys it), a secondary sale (if the company permits it), or a tender offer (the company or an investor offers to buy back shares at a set price).

### Why It Matters

The illiquidity is actually a big deal for compensation. If you're evaluating a job offer with equity in a private company, that equity is worth substantially less than the same dollar amount in public stock or cash — because you can't access it, you don't know when (or if) you'll be able to, and the valuation might not hold. A common rule of thumb is to discount private equity by 50% or more when comparing offers, though the right discount depends heavily on the company's stage and trajectory.

## Stock Compensation

Stock compensation is how companies pay employees with company shares, in addition to salary / cash. This is common at startups and tech companies as a way to conserve cash while giving employees ownership stakes.

- This also incentivizes employees to care a little bit more than at traditional companies where you're only getting salary. The better the company is doing, the better the stock will do and the more money you'll get out of your stock compensation.

Stock Options - you get the right to buy company shares at a fixed price (the strike price) for a certain period. If the stock price goes above your strike price, you can exercise the options and buy shares cheaply, and sell them at market price for profit. If the stock never rises above your strike price, the options expire worthless.

Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) - These are actual shares given to you, with restrictions. You dont own them immediately, they vest over time (typically 3-4 years). Once vested, you own the shares outright and can sell them. This has particular advantages because even if the stock price falls, you're still getting actual shares for free.

- Often the smartest approach here is immediately selling the vested shares, don't try to time the market and think you can get a bit more value of it.

Vesting works by incentivizing employees to stick around. A common schedule is 25% after year 1, then monthly after that.

Tax implications

- Options - you generally pay taxes when you exercise (buy the shares), not when granted
- RSUs - you pay ordinary income tax when shares vest (regardless of whether you sell or not), as if you received cash salary equal to the share value
  - So if you have $100k in RSUs that vest, and you're at the 37.5% tax bracket, you'll have to pay $37,500 in tax.
  - If you hold the stock and it drops to $80,000 in value by tax time, you still owe taxes on the full $100,000 but now need to sell more shares (or find other cash) to pay the tax bill.
  - This is why RSU tax planning is so important, especially at higher income levels. Trying to time or game the system could mean tens of thousands in additional tax that you have to come up with if things don't go your way.

## Dual Class Shares

Dual class shares are a structure where a company issues different types of stock with unequal voting rights, even though they represent ownership in the same company.

Typically there are two classes - Class A and Class B shares.

- Class A might have 1 vote per share, while Class B has 10 votes per share (or sometimes no voting rights at all).
- Both classes usually have equal claims on dividends and company assets.

So when these comapnies IPO, it opens them up to receiving funding from stockholders that want to buy shares on the stock market, but it still allows power to remain with individuals within the company through these class b shares which hold more weight and arent avaialble to the public.

- Google, Facebook, and Berkshire Hathaway are all structured like this

The tradeoff for investors is you can still invest in successful companies, but you have little say in major decisions. The founders maintain control even as the company grows and takes on more public investors.

## Exchanges

A stock exchange is essentially a regulated marketplace that matches buyers and sellers of securities. The NYSE and NASDAQ are the two biggest in the US. Each company that wants to trade publicly lists on a specific exchange and has to meet that exchange's requirements around financials, governance, and reporting.

Historically, exchanges were physical places — the NYSE trading floor with people shouting orders is the iconic image. Today they're almost entirely electronic matching engines. When you place an order, it gets routed to the exchange, matched against the best available counterparty, and executed — usually in milliseconds.

### Do You Have to Use an Exchange?

Yes and no.

You as a retail investor will almost always go through a broker (Fidelity, Schwab, Robinhood, etc.), and your broker routes your order to be executed. But here's where it gets interesting — your order doesn't necessarily go to the exchange where the stock is listed.

Alternative venues handle a huge portion of US equity trading:

Dark pools are private trading venues run by banks and firms like Credit Suisse or Goldman Sachs. They match large institutional orders away from public exchanges to avoid moving the market price. If a pension fund wants to sell 5 million shares of Apple, doing it on the open exchange would tank the price before they finish. Dark pools let them find a counterparty more discreetly.

Wholesale market makers like Citadel Securities and Virtu Financial are where a lot of retail orders end up. When you buy a stock on Robinhood, your order is very likely routed to one of these firms through a practice called payment for order flow (PFOF). The market maker pays your broker a small fee for the right to fill your order, and in theory gives you a price at or slightly better than what's available on the exchange. This has been controversial — it's how Robinhood makes money, and critics argue it creates misaligned incentives.

ECNs (Electronic Communication Networks) are another category of alternative trading venue that automatically matches orders.

All of these venues are regulated by the SEC and must comply with rules like the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO), which requires that your order be executed at the best available price across all venues. So even though your order might not touch the actual NYSE, you're supposed to get a price at least as good as what's posted there.

### Why This Matters

The short answer is that the exchange is the backbone — it sets the reference price and provides transparency — but the actual execution ecosystem is much more complex than "buyer meets seller on the NYSE." For a regular investor buying normal quantities, the practical difference is minimal. You place an order through your broker and it gets filled at or near the quoted price. The plumbing underneath is complicated, but it mostly works in your favor through competition between venues.

## Funds

A fund is a pooled investment vehicle — a bunch of people put money into a single pot, and that pot buys a collection of assets according to some strategy or mandate. Instead of buying 50 individual stocks yourself, you buy shares in a fund that holds those 50 stocks. You own a proportional slice of everything in the fund.

### Why Funds Exist

They solve several problems at once.

Diversification is the big one. Owning a single stock is risky — one bad earnings report and you're down 30%. But if you own 500 stocks, any single company's bad day barely registers. Funds give you instant diversification without needing the capital or effort to buy hundreds of positions yourself.

Access and convenience matter too. Most people don't have the time, knowledge, or interest to research individual stocks, rebalance a portfolio, and manage tax implications. Funds handle all of that. You make one purchase and get a professionally managed, diversified portfolio.

Economies of scale mean funds can trade at lower costs than individuals. They also get access to asset classes that would be hard to buy directly — like bonds, international markets, real estate, or commodities.

### Types of Funds

Mutual funds are the traditional structure. You buy in at the end-of-day price (called the NAV — net asset value), and the fund manager buys and sells holdings according to the fund's strategy. These can be actively managed (a human picking stocks trying to beat the market) or passively managed (tracking an index).

Index funds are a subset of mutual funds that simply track a benchmark like the S&P 500. They don't try to pick winners — they just own everything in the index in proportion to its weight. The insight behind them, popularized by Jack Bogle and Vanguard, is that most active managers fail to beat the index after fees over long periods. So why pay for stock picking when you can just own the market cheaply? Index fund expense ratios can be as low as 0.03% annually, compared to 0.5-1.5% for actively managed funds. That fee difference compounds enormously over decades.

ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) are similar to mutual funds in what they hold, but they trade on an exchange like a stock throughout the day. You can buy and sell them at any point during market hours at the current market price, rather than waiting for end-of-day NAV pricing. Most ETFs are passively managed and track indexes, though actively managed ETFs exist too. They also tend to be more tax-efficient than mutual funds due to how they handle redemptions.

Target date funds are a "set it and forget it" option. You pick a fund based on your expected retirement year (like a 2055 fund), and it automatically shifts from aggressive (more stocks) to conservative (more bonds) as you get closer to that date. These are extremely common as the default option in 401k plans.

Other types include bond funds, sector funds (just tech, just healthcare), international funds, REIT funds (real estate), and money market funds (very short-term, low-risk debt that functions almost like a savings account).

## Gambling

### Short Selling

Short selling is the more straightforward concept, even though the mechanics are a bit weird.

Normally you buy low and sell high. Short selling reverses the order — you sell high first, then buy low later. Here's how it actually works: you borrow shares from your broker (they lend them from another client's account), immediately sell those borrowed shares on the open market at the current price, and then at some later point you buy shares back on the open market to return what you borrowed. If the price dropped, you pocket the difference. If it went up, you're losing money.

Say you short 100 shares of a company at $50. You get $5,000 from selling them. If the stock drops to $30, you buy 100 shares back for $3,000, return them to your broker, and keep the $2,000 difference. But if the stock goes to $80, you have to buy back at $8,000, losing $3,000.

The critical thing about shorting is that your downside is theoretically unlimited. A stock can only go to zero (you'd make 100%), but it can go up infinitely. If you short a stock at $50 and it runs to $500, you're down 900%. This is what happened with GameStop in 2021 — short sellers got caught in a "short squeeze" where the price kept rising, forcing them to buy back at increasingly terrible prices, which pushed the price even higher in a feedback loop.

You also pay ongoing costs while short. You owe interest on the borrowed shares, and you're responsible for paying any dividends the stock issues while you're borrowing it. Your broker can also force you to close the position (a margin call) if the trade moves against you enough.

## Options

Options give you the right, not the obligation, to buy or sell a stock at a specific "strike" price.

The strike price is just the predetermined price written into the any options contract at which the transaction will happen if the option is exercised.

Think of it as the "locked in" price. When you buy a call with a $55 strike, you're buying the right to purchase shares at $55 no matter what the market price is. When you buy a put with a $45 strike, you're buying the right to sell shares at $45 no matter what the market price is.

Every options contract has an expiration date. After that date, the contract ceases to exist. You pick the expiration when you buy the option, just like you pick the strike price, and there are multiple expirations available at any given time.

### Put Options

A put option is a contract that gives you the right, but not the obligation, to sell a stock at a specific price (the strike price) before a specific date (the expiration). You pay a premium upfront for this contract.

Say a stock is trading at $50 and you buy a put with a $45 strike price expiring in 3 months, and the premium is $2 per share (options trade in contracts of 100 shares, so that's $200 total). You now have the right to sell 100 shares at $45 no matter what happens. If the stock drops to $30, your put is worth $15 per share (the difference between the $45 strike and the $30 market price) — so your contract is worth $1,500, minus the $200 you paid, netting $1,300 profit.

If the stock stays above $45 or goes up? Your put expires worthless and you lose the $200 premium. That's it. That's the maximum you can lose.

This is the key advantage of puts over shorting: your risk is capped at the premium you paid. You can never lose more than that. With a short, the losses can keep growing as long as the stock keeps rising.

### Call Options

A call gives you the right, but not the obligation, to buy a stock at a specific strike price before expiration. It's the mirror image of a put — you're betting the stock goes up instead of down.

### Short Selling vs Put Options

Short selling gives you dollar-for-dollar exposure to a stock's decline, but with unlimited risk, margin requirements, and borrowing costs. It's a blunt instrument.

Puts give you leveraged, defined-risk exposure. You pay a relatively small premium to control downside exposure on a much larger position. But you're also fighting time decay — options lose value as they approach expiration because there's less time for the expected move to happen. If you're right about the direction but wrong about the timing, you can still lose everything.

Hedging is the original reason these instruments exist. If you own a large position in a stock and you're worried about a short-term pullback, buying a put is like buying insurance — you pay a small premium to protect against a big loss. Institutional investors do this constantly.

Portfolio management uses shorts and puts to reduce overall market exposure. A fund might be long on stocks it likes and short on ones it thinks are overvalued, trying to profit from the *relative* performance rather than the market direction.

Price discovery is a more abstract but important function. Short sellers often uncover fraud and overvaluation. Some of the biggest corporate scandals — Enron, Wirecard, Luckin Coffee — were first flagged by short sellers who did deep research and put real money behind their conviction that something was wrong.

So they're tools. Like any tool, they can be used thoughtfully or recklessly. The "gambling" version is when people use them with high leverage and short time horizons purely on directional bets, which is what a lot of retail options trading on platforms like Robinhood amounts to.

## Company Analysis

Technical vs fundamental analysis are two different schools of evaluating stocks.

Fundamental analysis looks at the business itself — revenue, earnings, growth, competitive position, management.

Technical analysis looks at price charts, patterns, and trading volume to predict future price movement. Fundamentalists think technicals are basically astrology; technicians think fundamentals are already priced in and patterns reveal market psychology.

Most serious institutional investors lean fundamental, though many use some technical analysis for timing entries and exits.
