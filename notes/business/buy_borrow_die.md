# Buy Borrow Die

This strategy is also called "Securities-Based Lending" or "Asset-Based Lending" and is widely used by the ultra-wealthy to access cash without triggering capital gains taxes.

Let's say you're a billionaire with $10 billion in stock. You want $100 million to buy a yacht, mansion, whatever.

Option A (Sell shares): You sell $100 million in stock. If your cost basis is near zero (common for founders), you owe ~23.8% federal capital gains tax plus state taxes. In California, that's potentially $37+ million in taxes. You net maybe $63 million.

- Cost basis means what you originally paid for the shares. If you bought at $1/share and it's now $100/share, your gain is $99/share and you owe tax on that gain.
- This is common at startups where founders get stock for very little money initially or $0, and then sell later on at a higher valuation

Option B (Borrow): You go to Goldman Sachs and say "I'd like a $100 million loan, secured by my shares." They happily oblige at maybe 3-5% interest because the collateral is rock solid. You get your $100 million, pay zero taxes (loans aren't income), and just pay interest annually, which might even be deductible in some cases.

This is critical to understand:

- Option A: You get $63 million after taxes and now you have $100 million less in stock.
- Option B: You only pay $5 million in interest, you get the full $100 million, and still keep all of your stock. And your stock might even grow in that year faster than the 5% loan, so your net worth increases!

The system is fundamentally flawed and biased towards capital owners. By borrowing against your assets instead of selling them, you can access vast sums of money without triggering capital gains taxes.

- Not to mention the billionaries also get the most favorable loan terms, lower rates, better collateral treatment, etc. than average people because the bank wants their business (both current and future).

You can keep rolling these loans indefinitely as long as your assets appreciate faster than the interest rate (historically very achievable with equity returns).

The "Die" part: When you die, your heirs inherit the stock with a "stepped-up basis." This means the cost basis resets to the value at your death. All those unrealized gains from your lifetime? Never taxed. Your heirs can sell immediately and owe nothing, or continue the borrow strategy themselves.

This is how billionaires can report tiny incomes (Bezos famously qualified for child tax credits some years) while living lavish lifestyles.

## Capital Gains Taxes

Capital gains tax *replaces* ordinary income tax on investment gains, you don't pay both on the same money.

Federal rates for long-term gains (assets held >1 year):

- 0% if your income is low
- 15% for most people
- 20% for high earners
- Plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax for high earners = 23.8% max federal

State taxes: Yes, most states tax capital gains, and many (including California) tax them as ordinary income. So California adds another 13.3% for high earners.

Short-term gains (held \<1 year): Taxed as ordinary income—no preferential rate.

Compare this to a lottery winner: they pay ordinary income rates (~37% federal + 13.3% state) because lottery winnings are taxed as regular income, not capital gains. If billionaires' wealth gains were taxed the same way, the rates would be roughly double what they currently pay when they *do* sell.

## Why the System Stays this Way

Political influence: Wealthy individuals and corporations spend heavily on lobbying and campaign contributions. The people who write tax laws are heavily influenced by those with the most to lose from changing them. Estate tax repeal, capital gains preferences, carried interest loopholes—all have well-funded defenders in Washington.

Complexity favors the wealthy: The tax code is thousands of pages long. Billionaires employ armies of lawyers, accountants, and wealth managers who specialize in finding and exploiting every legal avenue. Average people can't afford this infrastructure, so they pay the "sticker price" while the wealthy negotiate and find the loopholes to exploit.

The "job creator" narrative: There's a genuine ideological argument (whether you buy it or not) that taxing capital more heavily discourages investment, slows economic growth, and ultimately hurts workers. This view has significant political power, especially in the Republican party but also among some Democrats.

Liquidity and practicality arguments: Taxing unrealized gains creates real implementation challenges.

- If Elon Musk owes $20 billion in taxes on paper gains, does he have to sell Tesla stock to pay it? What if the stock crashes the next year, does he get a refund?
- What about someone whose wealth is a private business or farmland that's hard to value?
- These aren't unsolvable problems, but they provide political cover for inaction.

The system was designed by and for capital owners: Historically, the people who wrote tax laws were themselves landowners and capital holders. Preferential treatment for investment income versus labor income is baked deep into the system's DNA.

ProPublica's analysis of leaked IRS data found that the wealthiest 25 Americans paid a "true tax rate" of about 3.4% when you compare taxes paid to wealth growth. Meanwhile most citizens pay >= 40% on their labor income. The system isn't broken, it's working exactly as designed for the people who designed it.
