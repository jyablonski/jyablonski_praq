# Home Buying

### Costs Associated with Buying a Home  
Buying a home involves several costs beyond just the purchase price. Here’s a breakdown:

#### 1. Upfront Costs  
- Down Payment: Typically ranges from 3% to 20% of the home price, depending on the loan type and your credit profile.  
- Earnest Money Deposit: A good faith deposit (1-3% of the home price) to show the seller you’re serious. This is applied to your down payment or closing costs.  

#### 2. Closing Costs (Typically 2-5% of the Home Price)  
- Loan Origination Fee: Charged by lenders for processing the mortgage (0.5% – 1% of the loan amount).  
- Appraisal Fee: Assesses the home's value (usually $300 – $600).  
- Home Inspection: Ensures the home is in good condition ($300 – $500).  
- Title Search & Title Insurance: Ensures no ownership disputes ($500 – $1,500).  
- Recording Fees & Transfer Taxes: Paid to local government ($100 – $2,000, varies by location).  
- Attorney Fees (if required in your state): $500 – $1,500.  
- Prepaid Property Taxes & Homeowners Insurance: Often required to fund an escrow account.  

#### 3. Ongoing Homeownership Costs  
- Mortgage Payments: Includes principal, interest, property taxes, and insurance (PITI).  
- Property Taxes: Varies by location, typically 0.5% to 2% of the home's value annually.  
- Homeowners Insurance: Typically $1,000 – $2,500 per year.  
- Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI): If your down payment is less than 20%, you may need PMI, which costs 0.3% – 1.5% of the loan annually.  
- HOA Fees: If your home is in a homeowners association ($100 – $500 per month).  
- Maintenance & Repairs: Budget 1-3% of home value per year.  

---

### First-Time Home Buyer Benefits  
As a first-time homebuyer, you may qualify for various financial assistance programs and tax benefits:

#### 1. Low Down Payment Loan Programs  
- FHA Loan: Requires only 3.5% down with a credit score of 580+.  
- Conventional 97 Loan: Allows 3% down for first-time buyers with good credit.  
- VA Loan (for Veterans): Zero down payment for eligible military service members.  
- USDA Loan: Zero down payment for homes in rural areas.  

#### 2. Down Payment Assistance Programs (DPA)  
Many states and local governments offer grants or low-interest loans to cover down payment and closing costs.  

#### 3. First-Time Home Buyer Tax Benefits  
- Mortgage Interest Deduction: Deduct mortgage interest from taxable income.  
- First-Time Home Buyer IRA Withdrawal: Withdraw up to $10,000 from an IRA penalty-free.  
- First-Time Home Buyer Tax Credit (pending new legislation): Potential $15,000 tax credit (check current federal programs).

## What Mortgage Rate Actually Means

Mortgage Rate dictates how much in annual interest you're paying based on the remaining balance of your loan. For example:

- With a 500k Loan on a 6% interest rate, you'd pay approximately 30k in interest that first year
- $500k  1.06
- But, you keep making monthly mortgage payments which are made of principal amounts + interest amounts, so your loan amount slowly goes down over time and thus also the amount of interest you're paying

Low rates are around 3-4%, high rates are around 7-8%

- A 3.5% rate pays 1/2 as much in interest as one w/ a 7% rate

### Example: $500,000 Loan, 30-Year Fixed Mortgage  

| Interest Rate | Monthly Payment (P&I) | Total Interest Paid Over 30 Years |
|--------------|----------------------|--------------------------------|
| 3.5%    | ~$2,245              | ~$308,000                     |
| 7.0%    | ~$3,326              | ~$697,000                     |

At 7%, you pay about $389,000 more in interest over 30 years—more than double the interest cost of a 3.5% loan


### 1. "Golden Handcuffs" Effect – Nobody Wants to Sell  
- During 2020-2021, mortgage rates were at historic lows (2.5%-3.5%). Homeowners who locked in those rates have no incentive to sell, because:  
  - If they buy a new house now, they’ll have to take on a 6-7% mortgage rate, doubling their monthly payment for the same loan amount.  
  - Even if they want to move, the higher cost makes it financially painful.  
- This means very few existing homes are hitting the market, which shrinks supply and keeps prices artificially high.  

### 2. First-Time Buyers Are Stuck  
- New buyers are getting hit with high prices + high mortgage rates, making homeownership unaffordable for many.  
- A $500K home that was affordable at 3% ($2,108/month) is now nearly $3,300/month at 7% — a 50%+ jump in payments.  
- Fewer buyers = slower home sales, which ripples through the economy.
