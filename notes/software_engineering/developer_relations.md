# Developer Relations (DevRel)

## What It Is

Developer relations is the practice of building and maintaining the relationship between a company and the developers who use, or could use, its products. It sits at the intersection of engineering, marketing, product, and community.

The function exists because certain products are adopted by developers making technical decisions rather than by executives responding to a sales pitch. When a developer reads the docs, writes a quick integration, and forms an opinion in the first fifteen minutes, DevRel is the team responsible for making that experience good.

## Core Responsibilities

Developer advocacy means representing developers' interests internally by feeding real usage friction back to product and engineering, while representing the company externally through conference talks, blog posts, and example projects.

Developer education covers documentation, tutorials, sample code, reference architectures, and increasingly video and interactive content. For a developer product, the docs are arguably the single most important surface.

Community building involves running or participating in forums, Discord and Slack communities, Stack Overflow, and GitHub discussions, along with organizing meetups and hackathons.

The feedback loop function synthesizes what developers struggle with and turns it into product requirements, API improvements, and documentation fixes.

## Common Titles

The field splits into a few recognizable roles. Developer Advocate and Developer Evangelist are outward facing, heavy on content, speaking, and community. Developer Experience engineers are more inward facing, focused on SDKs, sample apps, and onboarding quality. Technical Writers and Documentation Engineers own the docs. Community Managers handle the day to day of forums and social spaces. A Head of DevRel or DevRel Manager owns strategy and metrics.

## Why Companies Support It

The deciding factor is whether developers self-serve their way to adoption through technical channels. When adoption is bottoms-up and a developer evaluates the product by reading docs and writing code, a smooth developer experience directly determines whether they stay. That makes DevRel a genuine driver of adoption rather than a cost center, even though the attribution is fuzzy.

That fuzziness is the perennial tension in the field. DevRel is hard to tie cleanly to revenue, so teams track a funnel of adoption metrics: signups, time to first successful API call, documentation engagement, SDK downloads, and eventual conversion to paid usage. Because attribution is imperfect, DevRel is often among the first teams trimmed during cost cutting, even when it is working.

## Which Companies Want It

Companies that need DevRel sell API-first or infrastructure products where developers are the buyers or primary users. Payment APIs, cloud platforms, databases and data infrastructure, developer tools, and open-source-core companies all fit. Stripe is the canonical example of DevRel done well, and Twilio built much of its early growth on developer-first outreach.

Companies that generally do not need it are those where the product is not consumed by developers, or where the buyer is a non-technical decision maker. HR software, consumer apps, and traditional enterprise SaaS sold top-down through a sales team have no developer audience to cultivate. Workday is a reasonable example: the buyer clicks through a UI, and there is no integration a developer forms an opinion about.

The rough test is whether developers independently discover, evaluate, and adopt the product through technical channels. If yes, DevRel matters a lot. If the product reaches its users through sales or a non-technical interface, it usually does not.

## Internal vs External

External DevRel applies when the developer audience lives outside the company, as with Stripe or Twilio. But there is a growing internal version, usually called platform engineering or developer experience, that applies even to companies selling nothing to developers. Any large engineering org builds internal platforms, shared libraries, and paved-road tooling for its own engineers. Someone has to write the docs, run onboarding, evangelize the golden path, and gather feedback on why teams ignore the blessed tooling. That is the same function pointed inward.

The cleaner mental model is a grid. External developers plus a developer product gives you classic public DevRel. Internal developers consuming an internal platform gives you platform engineering. External non-developer buyers give you sales and marketing with no DevRel. No meaningful developer audience in either direction means the function does not exist.

## Value to Users

For developers, DevRel shows up as better documentation, faster and less painful integration, working sample code, responsive community spaces, and a real channel for their feedback to reach the product. When it is done well, the difference between a good and bad developer experience is measured in hours saved and frustration avoided during that first integration.
