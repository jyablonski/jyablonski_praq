# Circle of Fifths

The Circle of Fifths is essentially the Periodic Table for music. It is a visual representation of the relationship between the 12 tones of the chromatic scale, their corresponding key signatures, and the associated major and minor keys.

It is organized like a clock. At the very top (12:00) is C Major, which has no sharps or flats. From there, the circle splits into two directions.

______________________________________________________________________

### 1. The Right Side: Moving by Fifths (Sharps)

When you move clockwise from C, you are ascending by the interval of a Perfect Fifth (7 semitones). With every step you take, you add one Sharp (#) to the key signature.

- 12:00 - C Major: 0 Sharps.
- 1:00 - G Major: A perfect fifth up from C is G. It has 1 Sharp.
- 2:00 - D Major: A perfect fifth up from G is D. It has 2 Sharps.
- 3:00 - A Major: A perfect fifth up from D is A. It has 3 Sharps.

The Pattern: This continues until you reach the bottom of the circle. This is why it is called the "Circle of Fifths."

### 2. The Left Side: Moving by Fourths (Flats)

When you move counter-clockwise from C, you are descending by a fifth (which is the same as ascending by a Perfect Fourth). With every step, you add one Flat (b).

- 12:00 - C Major: 0 Flats.
- 11:00 - F Major: A perfect fourth up from C is F. It has 1 Flat.
- 10:00 - Bb Major: A perfect fourth up from F is Bb. It has 2 Flats.
- 9:00 - Eb Major: A perfect fourth up from Bb is Eb. It has 3 Flats.

______________________________________________________________________

### 3. The Order of Sharps and Flats

The Circle doesn't just tell you _how many_ sharps or flats are in a key; it tells you _which ones_ they are.

#### The Order of Sharps

The sharps are added in a specific order: F, C, G, D, A, E, B.

- _Mnemonic:_ Fast Cars Go Dangerously After Every Bend.
- The Sharps for each key are always in this order for the particular key, it's just n - 2 lagged.
- _Example:_ D Major has 2 sharps. They will be the first two in the sequence: F# and C# - it skips G# right before it and it doesn't include D#.
- _Example:_ E Major has 4 sharps. F#, C#, G#, and D#, skipping A# right before it.

#### The Order of Flats

The flats are added in the reverse order: B, E, A, D, G, C, F.

- _Mnemonic:_ BEAD Go Car Fast.
- No Flats are skipped; they are always the first n flats in this order.
- _Example:_ Eb Major has 3 flats. They will be Bb, Eb, and Ab.

______________________________________________________________________

### 4. The Inner Circle: Relative Minors

Most diagrams of the Circle of Fifths include a smaller inner ring. These are the Relative Minor keys.

- Every Major key has a "cousin" Minor key that shares the exact same key signature (same notes, different starting point).
- To find the relative minor, go down 3 semitones (or a minor 3rd) from the Major root.

Example:

- C Major (Outer circle) has no sharps/flats.
- Go down 3 semitones to A Minor.
- Therefore, A Minor is located directly under C Major on the inner ring.

______________________________________________________________________

### 5. Enharmonic Equivalents

At the bottom of the circle (6:00 position), you will see 3 enharmonic equivalents:

- Db / C#
- Gb / F#
- Cb / B

These keys sound the same but are written differently depending on the context (whether you are using sharps or flats).

- Typically, the key with fewer accidentals is preferred in notation.
- An accidental is just a sharp or a flat.

### Why is this useful?

1. Songwriting: Keys that are next to each other on the circle sound good together. If you are in C Major, chords from G Major (neighbor right) and F Major (neighbor left) fit perfectly.
1. Modulation: If you want to change keys in the middle of a song, it is smoothest to move to a neighbor on the circle because they share almost all the same notes.
1. Transposition: If a song is too high for a singer, you can move the whole song one "step" left on the circle to lower the pitch while keeping the harmonic relationships intact.
