# Music Theory Overview

## The 12 Chromatic Notes

There are only 12 distinct pitches in Western music:

1. A
2. A# (A sharp) / Bb (B flat)
3. B
4. C
5. C# (C sharp) / Db (D flat)
6. D
7. D# (D sharp) / Eb (E flat)
8. E
9. F
10. F# (F sharp) / Gb (G flat)
11. G
12. G# (G sharp) / Ab (A flat)

## Sharps and Flats

- Sharps (#) raise a note by a half-step: C#, D#, F#, G#, A#
- Flats (flat) lower a note by a half-step: Dflat, Eflat, Gflat, Aflat, Bflat
- Enharmonic equivalents: A# = Bflat, F# = Gflat, etc.
- No sharps/flats exist for: B#, E# (would just be C, F) and Cflat, Fflat (would just be B, E)

## Drop B Tuning

- Standard: E-A-D-G-B-E
- Drop B: B-F#-B-E-G#-C#
- First 5 strings tuned down 1.5 semitones, low string down 2.5 semitones
- Creates octave relationship between 6th and 4th strings for easy power chords

## Power Chords

- Two notes: Root + Perfect 5th (5 notes apart, 7 actual semitones apart)
- Examples: A5 = A + E, C5 = C + G
- No 3rd interval = neutral sound (neither major nor minor)
- In drop tuning: one finger across multiple strings creates full power chord with octave

### Extended Power Chords

Extended Power Chords are power chords that are played w/ additional octaves of the root or even of fifth being played

- So instead of playing a 1 finger power chord on 2 strings, you can play the same 2 notes across 5+ strings for a thicker, fuller sound
- They're not major or minor, they're still power chords

For example, you can play any of the 12 power chords on the neck w/ this shape:

```
+2 frets
+2 frets
+2 frets
+4 frets
+5 frets

Low 3 strings: same fret
3rd string: +2 frets
2nd string: +3 frets (from the root)
```

All 12 power chords in Drop D:

- Fret 0: D5 (0-0-0-2-3)
- Fret 1: D#5/Eb5 (1-1-1-3-4)
- Fret 2: E5 (2-2-2-4-5)
- Fret 3: F5 (3-3-3-5-6)
- Fret 4: F#5/Gb5 (4-4-4-6-7)
- Fret 5: G5 (5-5-5-7-8)
- Fret 6: G#5/Ab5 (6-6-6-8-9)
- Fret 7: A5 (7-7-7-9-10)
- Fret 8: A#5/Bb5 (8-8-8-10-11)
- Fret 9: B5 (9-9-9-11-12)
- Fret 10: C5 (10-10-10-12-13)
- Fret 11: C#5/Db5 (11-11-11-13-14)
- Fret 12: D5 (12-12-12-14-15) - back to D, one octave higher!

## Triad

3-note chord built by stacking two intervals of a third on top of each other

- Root, the foundation note
- Third, determines if it's major or minor
- Fifth, adds stability

- When we say "C Major", we actually mean "C Major Triad"
- Likewise when we say "C Minor", we actually mean "C Minor Triad"

## Major vs Minor Chords

### Major Chords

- Root + Major 3rd (4 semitones) + Perfect 5th
- Sound: bright, happy, resolved
- Example: C major = C + E + G

### Minor Chords

- Root + Minor 3rd (3 semitones) + Perfect 5th
- Sound: darker, sadder, melancholy
- Example: A minor = A + C + E

## Terminology

### Guitar Knobs

Volume Knob controls how much signal from your pickups goes to the amp. Turn it down and you send a weaker signal, which can clean up a distorted amp tone or just make you quieter overall. Some players "ride" this knob during songs to go from clean to dirty.

Tone Knob is a simple treble roll-off control - it's basically a high-frequency filter. All the way up (usually 10) lets all frequencies through. As you turn it down, it progressively cuts the high end, making your sound darker, warmer, or "muddier" depending on how far you go.

Key differences:

- Volume affects overall signal strength
- Tone only affects brightness/darkness by filtering highs
- The tone knob is passive (can only cut, never boost)
- Some guitars have separate tone knobs for different pickups, or one tone that affects both

### Amp

Gain/Drive controls how much the preamp section distorts your signal. Low gain gives you clean tones, while cranking it up adds overdrive and distortion. Think of it as how "dirty" your sound gets before it hits the power amp.

Volume/Master Volume

- Volume (or "preamp volume"): Controls the signal level going into the gain stage
- Master Volume: Controls the overall output level after all the tone shaping happens
- The interaction matters: high gain + low master = distorted tone at bedroom levels

Treble/Mid/Bass (EQ) controls that boost or cut specific frequency ranges. Treble adds brightness and cut, mids affect body and presence in the mix, bass adds thickness and low-end thump.

Presence adds extra high-end frequencies (usually around 4-6kHz) that occur after the tone stack. It's brighter than the treble control and adds "air" or "sizzle" to your sound. Great for helping your guitar cut through a band mix.

Resonance/Depth is the low-frequency counterpart to presence - adds bass frequencies after the tone stack. Makes the low end feel bigger and more "chest-thumping."

Reverb adds space and ambience to your sound, from subtle room reflections to big hall echoes.

### Tone Stack

The tone stack (also called the tone circuit or EQ section) is a specific part of the amplifier's circuitry where your treble, mid, and bass controls shape the frequency content of your signal.

Where it sits in the signal chain:
Guitar -> Input -> Preamp/Gain stages -> Tone Stack -> Master Volume -> Power Amp -> Speaker

Why it matters:
The tone stack is a passive circuit (in most amps), meaning it can only cut frequencies, not boost them. When you turn up the treble knob, you're actually cutting less treble rather than adding more. This is why the controls interact with each other - adjusting one affects how the others sound.

Different amp manufacturers use different tone stack designs, which is partly why a Fender, Marshall, and Vox sound different even with similar settings:

- Fender tone stack: Scooped mids, lots of bass and treble available
- Marshall tone stack: More mid-focused, the classic rock sound
- Vox tone stack: Called "Top Cut" - simpler, cuts treble only
- Baxandall: Active EQ that can actually boost (found in some modern amps)

Why Presence/Resonance are different:

- These controls come _after_ the tone stack in the power amp section, which is why they can add frequencies rather than just cutting them, and why they have a different character than your regular EQ knobs.

### Signal

When you pluck a string, the pickup generates a clean electrical waveform - if you visualized it, it would look like smooth, rolling sine waves. This is your "clean" signal.

Gain amplifies (boosts) that signal. The more you turn up the gain, the stronger that signal becomes. Here's the key: electronic components (tubes or transistors) can only handle signals up to a certain voltage before they max out.

- Some amps have boost knobs, which is basically adding more gain to push the amp into distortion
- A boost pedal increases the signal level before it hits the amp

When you boost the signal beyond what the circuit can handle, the peaks of those smooth waves get "clipped" - literally flattened off at the top and bottom. Instead of smooth curves, you get waveforms with flat tops, which creates additional harmonic frequencies that weren't in the original signal.

Think of it like this:

- Clean tone: smooth wave -> (fundamental frequency only)
- Distorted tone: clipped wave -> (fundamental + tons of harmonic overtones)

Those added harmonics are what we hear as "distortion," "overdrive," or "crunch." The more clipping, the more harmonics, the more "distorted" it sounds.

Different types of clipping:

- Soft clipping (tubes, overdrive pedals): Gradual rounding/compression = warm, musical overdrive
- Hard clipping (solid-state, distortion pedals): Sharp, sudden flattening = more aggressive, harsh distortion
