# Guitar Practice Techniques

## Fretting Hand Fundamentals

Chromatic exercises are a staple warmup. Play each fret on each string sequentially (1-2-3-4) up and down the neck. The goal isn't speed — it's clean, even notes with minimal finger lift. Once comfortable, try permutations like 1-3-2-4 or 4-2-3-1 to build independence between fingers.

Spider exercises take this further by having your fingers "walk" across strings in patterns, training your hand to stretch and move laterally while maintaining pressure. These are boring but genuinely effective for building dexterity you'll use everywhere else.

## Picking Hand Development

Alternate picking (strict down-up-down-up) is worth drilling deliberately. Many players develop a habit of all downstrokes early on, which caps your speed later. Practice it on single strings first, then across string changes where it gets tricky.

Economy picking blends alternate picking with sweep motions when crossing strings in the same direction. It's worth exploring once your alternate picking is solid.

Fingerpicking patterns like Travis picking (thumb alternates bass notes while fingers pick melody on higher strings) open up a whole world of solo guitar playing.

## Rhythm and Timing

Practice with a metronome — not just having one on in the background, but actively locking in with it. Start slow (60-70 BPM) and only increase tempo when you can play something perfectly at the current speed.

A great exercise: set a metronome to a slow tempo and try to place a single note exactly on each beat. Then try placing notes on the "and" (offbeat). Then try eighth notes, then sixteenth notes. This builds internal timing that makes everything else sound better.

Strumming patterns deserve isolated practice too. Work on common patterns (down, down-up, down, down-up for example) and focus on keeping your strumming arm moving consistently even when you skip a strum.

## Chord Work

Chord transitions are their own skill. Pick two chords you struggle to switch between and just go back and forth, slowly, focusing on all fingers landing simultaneously rather than one at a time. The "one-minute change" drill (count how many clean switches you can make in 60 seconds, then try to beat your score) is effective here.

Barre chords require dedicated practice if you haven't gotten comfortable with them yet. The key insight most people miss is that it's less about grip strength and more about positioning — rolling your index finger slightly onto its side and getting your elbow tucked in close to your body.

Once open and barre chords feel natural, learning chord voicings up the neck (triads on different string groups, jazz voicings, partial chords) expands your palette significantly.

## Scales and Theory Application

Learning the pentatonic scale (minor and major) in all five positions is foundational for soloing and improvisation. Rather than just running up and down the scale, practice making musical phrases with it — play three or four notes that sound like they "say something," pause, then play another phrase.

The major scale and understanding intervals gives you the framework to understand why chords are built the way they are and how to navigate the fretboard with intention rather than memorized shapes.

## Ear Training

Learning songs by ear (even partially) is one of the most valuable things you can do. Start with simple melodies you know well — pick out the notes one at a time. It's slow and frustrating at first but builds a connection between what you hear and what your hands do that tab-reading alone won't develop.

## Scales

- Major scale: W-W-H-W-W-W-H
- Minor scale: W-H-W-W-H-W-W
- Major Pentatonic: W-W-WH-W-WH
- Minor Pentatonic: WH-W-W-WH-W

WH means 3 frets

## Practice Structure

A common pitfall is noodling for 45 minutes and calling it practice. A more effective session might look like:

- 10 min - chromatic
- 10 min - spider walk
- 5 min - metronome
- 10 min - scale practice
- 10 min - learning new material
