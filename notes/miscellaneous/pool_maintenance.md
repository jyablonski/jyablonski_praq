# Pool Maintenance

## How Pools Stay Clean

Pools rely on a combination of circulation, filtration, and sanitation to stay clean. The pump pushes water through a filter (sand, cartridge, or DE) to remove physical debris, while chemicals handle the microscopic stuff — bacteria, algae, and organic contaminants from swimmers, leaves, sunscreen, etc.

The pump needs to be consistently running for the pool to not get dead zones where algae can take hold. The pump primarily just recirculates water, it's not adding to it. During warmer months, some water will evaporate over time, so it's normal to manually add fresh water to maintain the level.

Chlorine is the workhorse sanitizer. When added to water, it forms hypochlorous acid (HOCl), which kills bacteria and oxidizes contaminants by breaking down their cell walls. Chlorine exists in two main forms in your pool:

- Free chlorine — the active, available sanitizer ready to kill stuff
- Combined chlorine (chloramines) — chlorine that's already bonded with contaminants and is largely "spent." This is what causes the strong "chlorine smell" and red eyes (counterintuitively, that smell means you need *more* chlorine, not less)

Total chlorine = free + combined. You want most of your total chlorine to be free chlorine.

The goal is to add enough chlorine to kill algae and other bacteria, but not enough to damage pool surfaces or irritate the skin and eyes for swimmers.

## Optimal Chemical Ranges

| Chemical | Ideal Range | What It Does |
| ------------------- | ------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Free Chlorine | 1–3 ppm | Active sanitizer |
| Cyanuric Acid (CYA) | 30–50 ppm (outdoor) | UV stabilizer for chlorine |
| Total Alkalinity | 80–120 ppm | pH buffer |
| pH | 7.4–7.6 | Affects chlorine effectiveness, comfort |
| Calcium Hardness | 200–400 ppm | Prevents corrosion/scaling |

A few notes on each:

Chlorine — Below 1 ppm and you risk algae and bacterial growth; above 3 ppm and you start getting irritation and faster degradation of pool surfaces. Note that the *effective* chlorine level depends heavily on CYA (more on that below).

Cyanuric Acid (CYA) — This is sunscreen for your chlorine. Without it, UV destroys free chlorine in a couple of hours. With too much, chlorine becomes "locked up" and less effective at killing things. The general rule of thumb is free chlorine should be ~7.5% of CYA (so at 40 ppm CYA, you want ~3 ppm chlorine). Indoor pools don't need CYA at all.

Total Alkalinity — Acts as a buffer that keeps pH stable. Too low and pH bounces around wildly with any addition; too high and pH drifts upward and gets hard to lower. Adjust alkalinity *before* fine-tuning pH.

pH — A measure of how acidic or basic the water is, on a 0–14 scale (7 is neutral). Pool water is slightly basic on purpose. Below 7.4, water becomes corrosive — it eats away at metal fittings, heater elements, and plaster, and stings eyes. Above 7.6, chlorine becomes dramatically less effective (at pH 8.0, only ~20% of your chlorine is the active hypochlorous acid form, vs ~50% at 7.5). High pH also causes scaling, cloudy water, and skin irritation. pH naturally drifts upward over time in most pools due to CO2 outgassing, aeration from features/jets, and the alkalinity of fill water — so you'll mostly be lowering it with acid rather than raising it.

Calcium Hardness — How much dissolved calcium is in the water, measured in ppm. Counterintuitive but important: water "wants" a certain amount of calcium and will take it from wherever it can get it. Too low (under ~150 ppm) and the water leaches calcium out of plaster, grout, and concrete surfaces — slowly destroying your pool's interior. Too high (over ~500 ppm) and calcium precipitates out as scale on tile, heater elements, and salt cells. Vinyl liner pools are more forgiving on the low end since there's no plaster to protect. Calcium hardness only goes up over time (from cal-hypo shock, fill water, etc.) — the only way to lower it is partial water replacement.

## Maintaining Chemical Balance

A practical weekly rhythm:

2–3x per week:

- Test free chlorine and pH (test strips are fine for routine checks; liquid drop kits like Taylor K-2006 are more accurate)
- Add chlorine as needed — liquid chlorine (sodium hypochlorite) or trichlor tablets in a floater/feeder are most common. Tablets add CYA over time, which matters for the next point.
- Empty skimmer/pump baskets, brush walls, vacuum if needed

Weekly:

- Test alkalinity, CYA (monthly is fine for CYA usually)
- Shock the pool — adding a high dose of chlorine (typically 10+ ppm) to break apart chloramines and burn off organic contaminants. Do this at dusk so UV doesn't immediately destroy it.
- Check pump/filter pressure; backwash or clean filter when pressure rises 8–10 psi above clean baseline

Adjustments cheat sheet:

- Raise chlorine -> liquid chlorine or cal-hypo (fast), tablets (slow)
- Lower chlorine -> wait it out, or partial drain
- Raise pH -> soda ash (sodium carbonate)
- Lower pH -> muriatic acid or dry acid (sodium bisulfate)
- Raise alkalinity -> baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)
- Lower alkalinity -> muriatic acid (also lowers pH, then aerate to bring pH back up)
- Raise CYA -> cyanuric acid stabilizer
- Lower CYA -> partial water replacement (no chemical removes it efficiently)

## Best Practices for Safety & Longevity

Chemical handling:

- Never mix chemicals together — especially chlorine products with acids. Chlorine gas is no joke.
- Always add chemical *to water*, never water to chemical
- Add chemicals to a running pool (circulating) and dose at the deep end, distributed around the perimeter
- Store chemicals in a cool, dry place, separated from each other

Don't swim right after:

- Shocking (wait until chlorine drops back below ~5 ppm)
- Adding acid (wait 20–30 min and let it circulate)
- Generally, retest before getting in

Pool longevity:

- Keep pH dialed in — low pH corrodes metal fittings, plaster, heater elements; high pH causes scaling and cloudy water
- Run the pump 8–12 hours a day in summer (less in winter); enough turnover that the entire pool volume cycles 1–2x daily
- Brush walls weekly, even if they look fine — prevents algae from getting a foothold in pores and grout
- Keep an eye on calcium hardness, especially with plaster pools — too low leaches calcium out of the surface
- Consider a winterization routine if you're in a freeze zone (not really an issue in OC, but worth noting)

Test honestly: the temptation is to assume everything's fine if the water looks clear. Algae can establish before you can see it, and CYA creep from tablet use is a slow killer of chlorine effectiveness. Replace test reagents yearly — they degrade.

A general rule: most pool problems are pH and CYA problems in disguise. If chlorine seems ineffective, check those two before assuming you need more chlorine.
