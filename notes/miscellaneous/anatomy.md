# Anatomy

## Organs

### The Brain and Nervous System

Your brain is the command center, controlling everything from voluntary movements to automatic functions like breathing. It processes information, stores memories, and generates thoughts and emotions. Your spinal cord and nerves transmit signals between your brain and the rest of your body.

- Cerebrum: Handles thinking, memory, and voluntary movement
- Cerebellum: Coordinates balance and fine motor control
- Brainstem: Controls automatic functions like breathing and heart rate
- Hypothalamus: Regulates temperature, hunger, thirst, and sleep cycles
- Pituitary gland: The "master gland" that controls other hormone-producing glands

### The Heart and Circulatory System

Your heart is a muscular pump that circulates blood throughout your body. It beats about 100,000 times a day, delivering oxygen and nutrients to tissues while removing waste products. Blood vessels (arteries, veins, and capillaries) form a network spanning about 60,000 miles in your body.

- Heart valves: Four valves (tricuspid, pulmonary, mitral, aortic) ensure blood flows in the right direction
- Aorta: The largest artery, carrying oxygen-rich blood from your heart to the body
- Vena cava: Large veins returning oxygen-depleted blood to the heart

### The Lungs and Respiratory System

Your lungs facilitate gas exchange - bringing oxygen into your bloodstream and removing carbon dioxide. Each breath you take involves millions of tiny air sacs called alveoli where this exchange happens.

- Trachea: Your windpipe, connecting your throat to your lungs
- Bronchi and bronchioles: Branching airways that distribute air throughout your lungs
- Diaphragm: The muscle below your lungs that contracts to pull air in
- Larynx: Your voice box, containing vocal cords

### The Liver

This is your body's chemical factory and detoxification center. Your liver processes nutrients from food, produces bile for digestion, stores energy, manufactures proteins for blood clotting, and filters toxins from your blood. It's remarkably resilient and can regenerate itself.

### The Kidneys

These bean-shaped organs filter your blood, removing waste products and excess fluids to produce urine. They also regulate blood pressure, maintain electrolyte balance, and produce hormones that control red blood cell production.

- Ureters: Tubes carrying urine from each kidney to the bladder
- Bladder: A muscular sac that stores urine
- Urethra: The tube through which urine exits the body

### The Stomach and Intestines

Your stomach breaks down food using acid and enzymes. Your small intestine (about 20 feet long) absorbs most nutrients, while your large intestine absorbs water and forms waste for elimination. The gut also houses trillions of beneficial bacteria crucial for digestion and immunity.

- Esophagus: The muscular tube connecting your mouth to your stomach
- Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile produced by the liver to help digest fats
- Appendix: A small pouch attached to your large intestine; its exact function is debated, but it may serve as a reservoir for beneficial gut bacteria
- Rectum and anus: The final portions of your digestive tract for waste elimination

### The Pancreas

This organ produces insulin and other hormones that regulate blood sugar levels. It also makes digestive enzymes that help break down proteins, fats, and carbohydrates.

### The Skin

Often overlooked, your skin is actually your largest organ. It protects against pathogens, regulates temperature through sweating, provides sensation, and produces vitamin D when exposed to sunlight.

### The Bones and Bone Marrow

Your skeleton provides structure, protects vital organs, and allows movement. Bone marrow inside your bones produces blood cells - red cells for oxygen transport, white cells for immunity, and platelets for clotting.

### The Immune System

This includes organs like your spleen, thymus, lymph nodes, and tonsils. Together they defend against infections, produce white blood cells, and help your body recognize and destroy harmful invaders.

- Spleen: Filters blood, removes old red blood cells, stores platelets, and helps fight certain infections
- Thymus: Located in your chest, it's where T-cells (important immune cells) mature; most active in childhood
- Lymph nodes: Small bean-shaped structures throughout your body that filter lymph fluid and trap bacteria and viruses
- Tonsils and adenoids: Tissues in your throat that help trap pathogens entering through your mouth and nose
- Bone marrow: Produces all your blood cells, including immune cells
- Peyer's patches: Lymphoid tissue in your small intestine that monitors gut bacteria

### The Endocrine Glands

Your thyroid regulates metabolism, your adrenal glands produce stress hormones, and your pituitary gland controls other hormone systems. These chemical messengers coordinate countless body functions.

- Thyroid: In your neck, regulates metabolism, energy, and calcium levels
- Parathyroid glands: Four tiny glands behind the thyroid that control calcium and phosphorus balance
- Adrenal glands: Sit atop each kidney; produce cortisol (stress hormone), adrenaline, and hormones affecting blood pressure and salt balance
- Pineal gland: Produces melatonin, regulating sleep-wake cycles
- Pancreas: Both an endocrine organ (insulin production) and digestive organ (enzyme production)

### Sensory Organs

- Eyes: Convert light into electrical signals for vision; include the cornea, lens, retina, and optic nerve
- Ears: Not just for hearing - the inner ear also controls balance through the vestibular system
- Tongue: Contains taste buds and helps with swallowing and speech
- Nose: Houses olfactory receptors for smell and warms/filters air you breathe

### Musculoskeletal Components

- Skeletal muscles: Over 600 muscles that create voluntary movement
- Tendons: Connect muscles to bones
- Ligaments: Connect bones to other bones
- Cartilage: Cushions joints and provides structure (like in your ears and nose)
- Synovial fluid: Lubricates joints to reduce friction

## Hormones and Chemical Messengers

Insulin and Blood Sugar Regulation:

- Insulin: Produced by beta cells in your pancreas, insulin acts like a key that allows glucose (sugar) from food to enter your cells for energy. Without it, sugar builds up in your bloodstream, leading to diabetes
- Glucagon: Also from the pancreas, it does the opposite of insulin - raises blood sugar when it drops too low by signaling your liver to release stored glucose
- Cortisol: Your adrenal glands produce this stress hormone, which also raises blood sugar and affects how your body uses fats, proteins, and carbohydrates

Other Key Hormones:

- Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4): Control your metabolic rate - how fast your body burns energy
- Growth hormone: Promotes growth in children and helps maintain muscle and bone mass in adults
- Testosterone: Primary male sex hormone, but present in everyone; affects muscle mass, bone density, and mood
- Estrogen and progesterone: Primary female sex hormones regulating reproduction, bone health, and cardiovascular function
- Adrenaline (epinephrine): The "fight or flight" hormone that increases heart rate and energy availability during stress
- Serotonin: Affects mood, sleep, appetite, and digestion (mostly produced in your gut)
- Dopamine: Involved in reward, motivation, and movement control
- Melatonin: Regulates your sleep-wake cycle
- Leptin and ghrelin: Control hunger and satiety signals

### Metabolism

Metabolism encompasses all the chemical reactions in your body that keep you alive:

Energy Production:

- Cellular respiration: Your cells convert glucose and oxygen into ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the energy currency your body uses for everything from muscle contraction to brain function
- Mitochondria: Often called the "powerhouses of the cell," these organelles within your cells produce most of your ATP
- Basal metabolic rate (BMR): The energy your body needs just to maintain basic functions at rest - breathing, circulation, cell production, and temperature regulation

Metabolic Processes:

- Catabolism: Breaking down molecules (like food) to release energy
- Anabolism: Building up molecules (like proteins for muscle) using energy
- Glycolysis: The breakdown of glucose for quick energy
- Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation: More efficient energy production processes in your mitochondria
- Ketosis: When carbohydrate intake is very low, your body breaks down fats into ketones for alternative fuel

Metabolic Regulation:

Your metabolism is influenced by thyroid hormones, insulin, cortisol, growth hormone, age, muscle mass, genetics, activity level, and even the temperature around you.

### Blood Components

Blood is a complex tissue with many crucial elements:

Blood Cells:

- Red blood cells (erythrocytes): Contain hemoglobin, which carries oxygen from lungs to tissues and returns carbon dioxide to the lungs
- White blood cells (leukocytes): Multiple types including lymphocytes (T-cells and B-cells), neutrophils, monocytes, eosinophils, and basophils - each fights infections differently
- Platelets (thrombocytes): Cell fragments that clump together to form clots and stop bleeding

Plasma Components:

- Plasma: The liquid portion of blood, about 55% of total blood volume
- Albumin: A protein that maintains blood volume and pressure
- Clotting factors: Proteins like fibrinogen that enable blood to clot
- Antibodies (immunoglobulins): Proteins that recognize and neutralize foreign invaders
- Electrolytes: Sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride - essential for nerve signals, muscle contraction, and fluid balance

### Enzymes and Proteins

Digestive Enzymes:

- Amylase: Breaks down starches (produced in saliva and pancreas)
- Pepsin: Breaks down proteins in the stomach
- Lipase: Breaks down fats (from pancreas)
- Lactase: Breaks down lactose (milk sugar) - some people lose this enzyme, causing lactose intolerance

Other Critical Proteins:

- Hemoglobin: Oxygen transport in red blood cells
- Collagen: The most abundant protein in your body, providing structure to skin, bones, tendons, and ligaments
- Keratin: Forms hair, nails, and the outer layer of skin
- Actin and myosin: Enable muscle contraction
- Antibodies: Part of immune defense

### Neurotransmitters

These chemical messengers enable nerve cells to communicate:

- Acetylcholine: Involved in muscle activation, memory, and learning
- GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid): The main inhibitory neurotransmitter, calming neural activity
- Glutamate: The main excitatory neurotransmitter, involved in learning and memory
- Norepinephrine: Affects alertness, arousal, and stress response
- Endorphins: Natural pain relievers and mood boosters

### Electrolytes and Minerals

These maintain crucial body functions:

- Sodium: Nerve signals, fluid balance, muscle contraction
- Potassium: Heart rhythm, muscle function, nerve signals
- Calcium: Bone strength, muscle contraction, nerve transmission, blood clotting
- Magnesium: Over 300 biochemical reactions including energy production and protein synthesis
- Iron: Essential component of hemoglobin for oxygen transport
- Zinc: Immune function, wound healing, DNA synthesis
- Iodine: Thyroid hormone production

### Vitamins

Organic compounds essential in small amounts:

- Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): Stored in body fat; important for vision, bone health, antioxidant protection, and blood clotting
- Water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C): Not stored, need regular intake; crucial for energy metabolism, red blood cell formation, and immune function

### pH Balance and Acid-Base Regulation

Your body maintains a very tight pH balance (around 7.4 in blood):

- Buffer systems: Chemical systems that prevent drastic pH changes
- Respiratory regulation: Breathing rate affects CO2 levels, which influences pH
- Renal regulation: Kidneys excrete or retain hydrogen ions and bicarbonate to maintain balance

## Energy Flow

### Carbs

1. You eat it -> Enzymes in your mouth and intestines break it down into glucose
2. Glucose enters your bloodstream -> Your blood sugar rises
3. Pancreas releases insulin -> Acts like a "key" to unlock your cells
4. Glucose enters cells -> Gets burned immediately for energy (ATP), stored in muscles/liver as glycogen, or converted to fat if you have excess

### Proteins

1. You eat it -> Stomach acid and enzymes break it down into amino acids
2. Amino acids enter bloodstream -> Circulate to where they're needed
3. Body uses them primarily for building -> Repairs muscle, makes enzymes, creates hormones, builds new tissue
4. If needed for energy -> Can be converted to glucose, then follows the same path as carbs (but this is not the preferred use)

### Fats

1. You eat it -> Bile from gallbladder breaks it into droplets, then enzymes break it into fatty acids
2. Fatty acids enter cells -> NO insulin needed (this is key!)
3. Burned for energy in mitochondria -> Produces even MORE ATP than glucose
4. Also used for -> Building cell membranes, making hormones, protecting organs
5. Excess stored as body fat -> Your long-term energy reserve

### Summary

- Carbs = Quick energy (needs insulin)
- Protein = Building blocks (used for repair first, energy second)
- Fat = Sustained energy + structural roles (doesn't need insulin)

Think of your body like a car:

1. Carbs = gasoline (fuel)
2. Protein = metal and parts (building materials)
3. Fats = oil and lubricants (essential functions)
4. Vegetables/Fruits = engine coolant, brake fluid, transmission fluid, spark plugs, filters, etc.

All three eventually produce ATP (the actual energy your cells use), but they take different routes to get there!

## Muscle Building

When you lift weights, you're creating microscopic tears in your muscle fibers. This sounds bad, but it's actually intentional damage - your muscles are being stressed beyond their current capacity.

After working out, your body recognizes this damage and thinks: "Okay, these muscles aren't strong enough for what this person is asking them to do. I need to repair them AND make them bigger/stronger so they can handle this stress next time."

The repair process looks like:

1. Your body sends amino acids (from the protein you eat) to the damaged muscle tissue
2. Muscle protein synthesis kicks in - your cells use those amino acids to repair the tears and build NEW muscle protein
3. Your muscles grow back slightly bigger and stronger than before - this is called hypertrophy
4. Over time, this accumulates - each workout, you damage -> repair -> grow slightly stronger

## Ketosis

When you stop eating carbs, your body freaks out. We have always relied on carbs to turn into glucose for energy to keep us going. But the body can actually produce ketones which is a different fuel source than glucose.

- Note: this is typically a lower-calorie diet.

When no or minimal carbs are being intaked, the body starts turning fat deposits into ketones which are used as a fuel source instead of glucose.

- This is an entirely different energy source than your body is used to
- At first, it takes you & your body some getting used to. First couple days you will have low energy and feel sluggish
- Eventually, you get used to it, and your energy stabilization really normalizes out compared to when you eat carbs and have highs and lows after you eat and rest.
- This can be great for weight loss because your body has to start breaking down fat that's been built up in order to function

On a carb-based diet:

- "I eat carbs -> they become glucose -> insulin helps glucose enter cells -> cells burn glucose for energy"

On a keto diet:

- "I eat (or burn) fat -> liver converts it to ketones -> ketones enter cells (no insulin needed) -> cells burn ketones for energy"

The small amount of glucose needed is made separately through gluconeogenesis, but it's not the main fuel source - ketones are.
